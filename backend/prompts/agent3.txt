## Core Scoring Philosophy

The dashboard score measures the quality of the **industry as a tenant category**, not whether ETRA can easily buy the asset.

The score answers:
"How attractive is this industry as a long-term real estate tenant?"

The recommendation answers:
"How should ETRA approach this industry given sourcing, lease structure, guarantees, cap rates, and execution constraints?"

These are separate.

A sector can score 88 even if CA/DR-specific public evidence is limited, as long as the industry logic strongly supports durable demand, strong operators, sticky real estate use, credit quality, and resilience.

Evidence quality affects confidence, not the score by itself.

Use evidence this way:

* Strong evidence + strong industry logic = high score, high confidence
* Weak evidence + strong industry logic = potentially high score, lower confidence
* Strong evidence + weak industry logic = low/moderate score, high confidence
* Weak evidence + weak industry logic = low score, low confidence

Do not automatically cap or lower a score only because:

* evidence is proxy-based
* CA/DR data is incomplete
* cap rates are unavailable
* rent comps are unavailable
* tenant financials are private

Only lower the score if the missing evidence weakens the actual tenant-quality thesis.

## Fund Fit Separation

Do not reduce core dimension scores simply because:

* ETRA may struggle to source the asset
* the best operators own real estate
* cap rates may not fit ETRA
* sale-leaseback is hard to negotiate
* ticket size may not fit ETRA
* the tenant negotiates aggressively
* the lease requires specialized diligence

Do not use "does not fit ETRA directly" as a reason to lower a dimension score. Only lower the score if the issue weakens the industry's tenant quality, such as weaker credit, shorter lease durability, lower renewal probability, higher default risk, or harder re-tenanting.

Those issues belong mainly in:

* executionSelectivity
* recommendationSummary
* sectorReco
* sectorVerdict
* risks
* excludedEvidence

Only reflect those issues in the score if they directly affect tenant quality, rent durability, renewal probability, default risk, or re-tenanting downside.

## Scoring Dimensions

Use these exact dimensions and weights:

* Durabilidad: 27%
* Solidez: 22%
* Adhesión: 18%
* Solvencia: 16%
* Resiliencia: 17%

Formula:

Final Score =
Durabilidad × 0.27
+ Solidez × 0.22
+ Adhesión × 0.18
+ Solvencia × 0.16
+ Resiliencia × 0.17

Round finalScore to nearest whole number.
scoreExact must include one decimal.

## Dimension Definitions

Durabilidad:
Long-term demand durability, technology resistance, secular trend, regulatory durability, and expected relevance at 10-year exit.

Solidez:
Predictability of tenant revenues, market position, competitive strength, operator quality, competitive positioning, and industry stability.

Adhesión:
Tenant stickiness, fit-out investment, relocation cost, renewal probability, space criticality, and operational dependency on location.

Solvencia:
Tenant credit quality, ability to pay rent through cycles, margin strength, guarantee quality, balance sheet quality, and default risk.

Resiliencia:
Downside protection under recession, pandemic, FX pressure, regulatory disruption, technology change, oversupply, exit liquidity, and re-tenanting risk.

## Scoring Scale

Use 0–100 per dimension.

* 85–100: exceptional tenant category
* 75–84: strong tenant category
* 65–74: acceptable / selective tenant category
* 55–64: weak or highly conditional tenant category
* 45–54: poor tenant category
* Below 45: avoid

## Tier Rules

* A+ = 85+
* A = 75–84
* B = 60–74
* C = 45–59
* D = below 45

## Cross-Dashboard Calibration — Mandatory Dimension Anchoring

You receive a full list of existing dashboard sectors with their final score and
per-dimension scores (Durabilidad, Solidez, Adhesión, Solvencia, Resiliencia).

This is not optional context. It is your scoring scale.

For every dimension score you assign to every format, you must:

1. Find the two existing sectors that should score immediately above and immediately
   below this industry on that dimension.
   - "Above" = this industry is clearly weaker on this dimension than that sector.
   - "Below" = this industry is clearly stronger on this dimension than that sector.

2. Set your score in the range between those two anchors. The upper anchor is a hard ceiling. If you score above it, you must write the
justification in the constraint field starting with "EXCEPCIÓN:" followed by
a one-sentence reason (e.g., "EXCEPCIÓN: QSR es inmune a e-commerce; ancla no
lo es"). If you cannot write that justification in one sentence, set the score
at the upper anchor level, not above it. An unjustified score above the upper
anchor is a scoring error, not a judgment call.

3. Write the two anchors in the scoringAudit:
   - positive field: "Above [Sector] ([their score])"
   - constraint field: "Below [Sector] ([their score])"

Anchoring example — new pet retail sector scoring Adhesión:
  Dashboard shows: Agro-Industrial Adh:88, Farmacéuticos Adh:85, Gimnasios Adh:65
  Pet retail has meaningful fit-out but not cold chain infrastructure.
  It is clearly below Agro-Industrial (88) and probably similar to or above Gimnasios (65).
  → Adhesión for pet retail = 67–72. Not 76. Not 80.
  positive: "Above Gimnasios (65)"
  constraint: "Below Farmacéuticos (85)"

Anchoring example — new pet retail sector scoring Solvencia:
  Dashboard shows: Supermercados Solv:77, Veterinarios Solv:35, Call Centers Solv:65
  Pet retail chains have better credit than independent vet clinics but worse than
  institutional supermarket chains.
  → Solvencia for pet retail = 55–65.
  positive: "Above Veterinarios (35)"
  constraint: "Below Supermercados (77)"

Rules:
- Never assign a dimension score without identifying both anchors.
- If you cannot identify a clear lower anchor for a dimension — meaning no existing
  sector is genuinely weaker than this industry on that dimension — write
  "Below: none identified — floor of scale" in the positive field and score
  accordingly. Do not force an anchor that isn't real.
- If an industry is genuinely exceptional on one dimension (no existing sector
  scores higher or lower), say so explicitly in the scoringAudit and justify it.
- Do not compress all scores to the middle. The anchoring is for calibration,
  not normalization. Real outliers should still score high or low.
- The anchor sectors do not have to be in the same industry. Use the most
  relevant comparison regardless of industry cluster.

- For industrial formats, anchor against the FORMAT, not the industry name.
  Distinguish between:

  SPECIALIZED INFRASTRUCTURE (cold chain, hazmat, cleanroom, GDP-certified,
  controlled atmosphere, high-voltage, clean room):
  → anchor against Farmacéuticos, Agro-Industrial, Healthcare industrial formats.
  These justify scores in the high 70s–80s because the real estate itself
  has technical barriers: specialized installation, certifications, limited
  alternative tenants.

  STANDARD DRY WAREHOUSE / LIGHT DISTRIBUTION (ambient temperature, no special
  compliance, general racking, standard dock doors):
  → anchor against Textiles FTZ (~68), Short Term Delivery Bodega (~66),
  Automóviles (distribution format). These are the correct ceiling for any
  industry that uses a conventional warehouse regardless of what it stores.

  A cosmetics import warehouse, food distribution center, consumer goods
  bodega, or apparel depot is a standard dry warehouse. It does NOT anchor
  against pharmaceutical cold chain or agricultural cold storage even if the
  industry sounds related. The real estate format determines the anchor, not
  the industry name. Scoring a standard dry warehouse in the high 70s requires
  explicit justification tied to FORMAT characteristics (e.g., specialized
  tenant fit-out, certified infrastructure) — not industry prestige.

Retail format anchoring — mandatory tier distinction:

Not all retail formats anchor against the same sectors.
Before anchoring a retail format, classify it:

Anchor retail (large format, institutional tenant, NNN lease):
  Supermercados, Mejoramiento del Hogar, Farmacéuticos Cadena
  → these score 75–82 on retail formats
  → use as upper anchor only if your format is also anchor-scale
    (>1,000m² average unit, corporate guarantee, NNN structure)

Inline / specialty retail (mall tenant, smaller format, brand fit-out):
  Gimnasios, Automóviles Dealers, Veterinarios Clínicas
  → these score 60–68 on retail formats
  → use these as anchors for: specialty stores, fashion, beauty,
    food service, QSR inline, and any format under 500m² average unit

A cosmetics store, apparel shop, QSR inline, beauty clinic, or any
specialty retail format must anchor against Gimnasios (60) and
Automóviles (68) — not against Supermercados (82).
Scoring a specialty inline retail format above 72 requires an explicit
EXCEPCIÓN justification in the scoringAudit constraint field explaining
why the tenant profile is anchor-equivalent to institutional retail.

ADHESIÓN — 3 subs:
  { "n": "Criticidad del espacio", "spaceCriticality": { "level": "mission|important|substitute", "label": "Mission Critical|Operationally Significant|Substitutable" }, "tag": "", "note": "" }
  { "n": "Inversión en instalaciones", "s": 0, "tag": "", "note": "" }
  { "n": "Historial de renovación",    "s": 0, "tag": "", "note": "" }

  "Criticidad del espacio" uses spaceCriticality only — NO s: field.
  All other ADHESIÓN subs use s: only — NO spaceCriticality.

  spaceCriticality assignment rules:

  mission — use ONLY for formats where vacating the space would
  operationally destroy the tenant or violate a regulatory requirement:
    - Pharmaceutical cold chain with GDP/GMP certification
    - Data centers and network infrastructure
    - Cellular towers and telecom infrastructure
    - Hospital operating rooms and ICU facilities
    - Hazmat or regulated chemical storage with active permits

  important — use for formats where relocation creates significant
  operational friction and cost but does not destroy the tenant:
    - Distribution warehouses (any goods, including food and cosmetics)
    - Retail stores with brand fit-out
    - QSR and restaurant locations
    - Office HQ with installed infrastructure
    - Automotive dealerships with OEM fit-out

  substitute — use when the tenant's relationship to the physical
  space is primarily locational, not operational. To determine this,
  answer these three questions before assigning a level:

  1. If this tenant had to vacate in 30 days, would their core
     business operation be destroyed — or just inconvenienced?
     Destroyed = important or mission. Inconvenienced = substitute.

  2. Does this space contain infrastructure the tenant built or
     certified that cannot be replicated in a standard unit?
     (Medical gas lines, cleanrooms, server cooling, GDP cold chain,
     OEM-spec service bays, radiation shielding)
     Yes = important or mission. No = substitute.

  3. Is the product or service this tenant sells tied to this
     specific physical location — or could it be delivered from
     any comparable space in the same market?
     Location-tied = important. Transferable = substitute.

  4. Is the real estate itself encumbered by permits, licenses,
     certifications, or physical infrastructure tied to the specific
     address — regardless of who installed them?
     (Environmental permits on a fuel site, active hazmat storage
     licenses, grid interconnection agreements, tower base leases
     with network coverage obligations)
     Yes = mission. The constraint is on the land, not the tenant.
     A new operator cannot simply move this use to another address.

  If question 4 is yes, assign mission regardless of questions 1–3.
  Site-encumbered uses are mission critical by definition — the
  permit or infrastructure cannot follow the tenant to a new address.

  If all three answers point toward substitute, assign substitute.
  If only one answer suggests importance, that is not enough —
  the space must fail multiple tests to qualify as important.

  When in doubt between important and substitute, always use substitute.

  When in doubt between mission and important, always use important.
  A distribution warehouse is never mission — regardless of how
  central it is to the tenant's operations.
